What Makes Eagle Mountain's Trees Different

The Wind Problem

Eagle Mountain sits on an elevated plateau with very little natural shelter from prevailing winds. Drive out on Eagle Mountain Boulevard on a blustery spring day and you'll feel it immediately — there's nothing blocking the wind from rolling across Cedar Valley. Trees planted in open front yards absorb all of that. Maples and elms with dense, full canopies act like sails, putting stress on the trunk and root system every time the wind picks up. We do a lot of crown thinning in Eagle Mountain for exactly this reason, and homeowners who maintain it consistently tend to avoid the broken limbs and toppled young trees that follow the city's bigger windstorms.

Species That Struggle Here

Aspens are probably the most commonly planted tree in Eagle Mountain that shouldn't be. They look great in the nursery and they're associated with Utah's mountains, so homeowners buy them — but Eagle Mountain's clay-heavy, alkaline soil, combined with its intense summer sun and low humidity, is genuinely hostile to aspen. Most of the aspens we see in Eagle Mountain yards are either already declining or surviving with constant intervention. Ornamental cherries have a similar problem. They bloom beautifully for a few springs and then start dropping branches or dying back on one side as the soil conditions wear them down. When we look at a distressed tree in Eagle Mountain, species mismatch is one of the first things we consider.

Dead Trees and Fire Risk

Eagle Mountain's location puts it in a drier, more exposed fire environment than most of Utah County. Dead trees near homes aren't just an eyesore here — they're a genuine fire fuel concern, particularly in the drier summer months when conditions across Cedar Valley can be severe. We consistently recommend addressing dead or mostly dead trees in Eagle Mountain more urgently than we might in a more sheltered city setting. If a tree in your yard is clearly dead or has lost more than half its canopy, it's worth getting it removed rather than watching it for another season. Our tree removal crew can usually schedule within a week or two for non-emergency situations.

Young Trees Planted Too Deep

During Eagle Mountain's rapid growth, trees were often planted by landscaping crews working quickly through large subdivisions. One of the most common mistakes in mass-production landscaping is planting trees too deep — burying the root flare, which is the widening at the base of the trunk where roots begin. Trees planted this way slowly decline over five to fifteen years as the buried bark rots and the root system fails to properly anchor the tree. If your tree has been in the ground for more than five years and you can't see any flare at the base — just a straight trunk disappearing into the soil — that may be worth looking at.
