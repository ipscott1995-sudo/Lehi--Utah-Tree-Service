Tree Problems That Are Common in Saratoga Springs

A few issues come up again and again when we work in Saratoga Springs, and they're worth knowing about as a homeowner.

Iron Chlorosis

If your maple or ornamental pear has leaves that are turning yellow while the veins stay green, it's almost certainly iron chlorosis. Saratoga Springs sits on highly alkaline clay soil — the same type that runs throughout the west side of Utah Lake — and that soil chemistry makes it hard for trees to absorb the iron they need. It's not a death sentence, but it does need treatment, and it tends to get worse every year without intervention. We run into this constantly in the older parts of Harvest Hills and along the neighborhoods closer to the lake.

Bradford Pear Failures

Bradford pears were the go-to ornamental tree for Utah developers through the 2000s and into the early 2010s. They're fast-growing, showy in spring, and cheap to install — which is exactly why they ended up in so many Saratoga Springs yards. The problem is their branch structure. Bradford pears tend to grow with multiple stems originating from the same point on the trunk, which creates weak connections that split under stress. Trees that were planted fifteen years ago are now large enough that when they fail — and they do fail, usually in late spring snowstorms or summer windstorms — they fail dramatically. If you have a Bradford pear with a trunk diameter over six inches, it's worth having someone look at it before next storm season.

Wind Load from Utah Lake

The west shore location means Saratoga Springs gets consistent wind, particularly from the southwest in spring and fall. Trees with dense, unpruned canopies catch a lot of that wind load, which puts stress on root systems and branch unions. Regular crown thinning makes a measurable difference — it's not just cosmetic. After major wind events, we typically spend several days in Saratoga Springs addressing limbs that came down on fences, roofs, and parked cars along streets like Triumph Boulevard and Freedom Boulevard.

Root Intrusion in Newer Subdivisions

Many Saratoga Springs homes were built with irrigation lines running through the top few inches of soil — which is exactly where tree roots expand as trees mature. By the time a maple or ash is ten to fifteen years old, its roots have often found those lines. We frequently get calls from homeowners who notice a soft or wet spot in their lawn and eventually trace it back to a root that's crushed or shifted a drip line. In some cases the solution is tree removal; in others, root pruning and a barrier can buy several more years. We'll give you a straight answer about which makes more sense.
