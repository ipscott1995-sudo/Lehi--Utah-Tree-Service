Tree Issues We See Most Often in Lindon

Lombardy Poplars at the End of Their Lifespan

Lombardy poplars were planted throughout Lindon and neighboring Pleasant Grove in the 1980s and 1990s — often in rows along property lines or at the back of lots to create a fast privacy screen. They served that purpose well. The problem is that Lombardy poplars are not a long-lived tree. Most have a natural lifespan of fifteen to twenty-five years, and many of the specimens in Lindon are well past that. They're also highly susceptible to Cytospora canker, a fungal disease that causes bark to die off in patches and branches to dieback from the top down. If you have a row of Lombardy poplars that are starting to look sparse or patchy, they're almost certainly in decline. The practical question is usually whether to remove them all at once and replace with something longer-lasting, or to take them out individually as they fail. We've had that conversation with a lot of Lindon homeowners and can help you think through what makes sense for your property.

Older Maples with Neglected Canopies

The established residential streets in Lindon have some genuinely beautiful maple trees — full canopy, good structure, decades of growth. Many of them haven't had professional attention in years, though, and it shows. Crossing branches, dead wood accumulating in the interior, water sprouts cluttering the crown. A proper crown cleaning and thinning on a mature maple can take years off how it looks and meaningfully extend its useful life. It's one of the most satisfying jobs we do — a well-maintained tree looks completely different from a neglected one, even though you've only removed maybe fifteen percent of the canopy.

Trees Outgrowing Their Planting Space

In Lindon's older neighborhoods, it's common to find trees that were planted thirty or forty years ago in spots that made sense then but are now a problem. A tree that was a small ornamental when planted near a fence line is now thirty feet wide. A maple that seemed like a good idea next to the driveway now has roots lifting the concrete every spring. When a tree has genuinely outgrown its space and is causing recurring damage, removal is usually the more cost-effective choice in the long run — especially when the alternative is repairing the same fence, driveway, or sidewalk every few years. We'll give you a straightforward read on that during your estimate rather than defaulting to the answer that earns us more work.
