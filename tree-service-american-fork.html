Working with American Fork's Mature Tree Canopy

American Fork is one of the older cities in Utah County, and it shows in its trees. Drive through the neighborhoods near 100 North or wind through the grid streets west of State Street and you'll find cottonwoods and silver maples that have been there for fifty or sixty years — trees with canopies that now shade the whole street, root systems that have long since found every water line in the area, and trunks measuring three or four feet across at the base.

That's a different kind of tree work than the young-subdivision jobs we do in newer cities. Removing or trimming a tree that's been in place since the 1960s requires different equipment, different climbing approaches, and a more careful read of the tree's structure and health. We've worked in American Fork long enough to know what to look for and how to plan around the constraints that come with large, established trees in tight residential lots.

Tree Issues Common in American Fork

Cottonwoods: The Trade-offs of a Great Tree

Cottonwoods were the go-to shade tree in central Utah for much of the twentieth century, and American Fork has a lot of them. They grow fast, they get enormous, and they're genuinely beautiful when healthy. They also have brittle wood that drops large limbs without much warning, root systems aggressive enough to crack concrete and invade water lines, and trunks that can develop hidden internal decay long before there's any visible sign of trouble from the outside. A cottonwood that looks full and healthy in June can surprise you in August when a major limb comes down. If yours hasn't been assessed in several years and it's within reach of your roof or driveway, it's worth having someone look at it. Our removal and trimming crews handle large cottonwoods regularly and know what to look for.

Silver Maples and Structural Problems

Silver maples are another species that was planted widely in American Fork decades ago. They grow fast and provide good shade, but they have a tendency to develop co-dominant stems — multiple large trunks growing from a single base — which creates a structurally weak union that can fail under snow load or high winds. Many silver maples in American Fork are now large enough that if a co-dominant stem does fail, it causes serious property damage. Cabling or bracing can sometimes reduce the risk without removing the tree; other times the better answer is removing the weaker stem or the whole tree. We'll give you an honest read during your estimate.

Trees and American Fork Creek

American Fork Creek and its corridor run through parts of the city, and the trees along those banks — cottonwoods, willows, box elders — can be substantial. Homeowners with property backing up to the creek sometimes need work done on trees that are leaning over the water or dropping large limbs onto the bank. This kind of job typically requires a site visit before we can quote it accurately, since access and the condition of the bank vary a lot from property to property.

Root Damage to Older Infrastructure

In the older parts of American Fork, tree roots and city infrastructure have been coexisting — and competing — for fifty-plus years. Lifted sidewalks, cracked curbs, and slow-draining sewer lines that turn out to have root intrusion are common. If you've had a plumber tell you there are roots in your sewer line, the tree supplying those roots is worth identifying and assessing. Sometimes the root can be cut back and a barrier installed; other times the math just favors removing the tree and grinding the stump to stop further root growth.

Occasional small branch drops are normal for cottonwoods, especially in summer heat — it's a self-pruning response to water stress. But if you're losing significant branches regularly, or if large limbs are coming down, that's worth investigating. It can indicate dead wood in the upper canopy, internal decay, root stress, or overcrowded branch structure.

A crown cleaning — removing dead, dying, and weakly attached branches — is often the right first step. It won't stop the tree from eventually dropping small twigs, but it removes the branches that actually pose a risk.

If you have ash trees, emerald ash borer (EAB) is worth knowing about. It's been moving westward across the US and Utah State University Extension has been tracking it closely as it approaches Utah. Preventive trunk injections are available and most effective before a tree shows symptoms — once EAB is established in a tree, treatment options narrow significantly.

For elm trees, Dutch elm disease is the longer-standing concern in Utah. It's spread by bark beetles and through root grafts between neighboring trees. If you have large elms and notice sudden wilting or branch dieback on one section of the canopy, get it looked at quickly — early detection is critical for DED management.
